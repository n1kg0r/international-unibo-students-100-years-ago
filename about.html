ABOUT PROJECT
For the project, we chose five Jewish students who studied at the University of Bologna between 1921 and 1939. Boris Epstein (Russia), Ibolya Barnai (Hungary), Szlama Josef Fromer (Poland), Anna Madorski (Manchukuo/Republic of China) and Mihaly Mestitz (Romania) came to Italy either to get education at the at the University on Bologna, or just to escape from their home countries.
Historical context of Italy during the first post-war period

On October 31st, 1922, Italy’s government was taken over by the National Fascist Party, guided by Benito Mussolini. This gave birth to the “fascist era” in Italian history, which formally ended on July 25th, 1943. Towards the end of these twenty years and just before the start of WWII, Italy became an ally of Nazi Germany with the Stahlpakt (Pact of Steel) signed on 22nd May 1939. This had been anticipated by a meeting of the Italian and the German foreign ministers in October 1938, during which it became clear that in the next years a conflict towards France and UK would have necessarily taken place.
1938 is also the year in which Italy first issues its racial laws. On 14th July 1938, the “Manifesto of Race” was published in “Il Giornale d’Italia”. This document is the ideological basis of the racial laws that were then implemented starting September that same year. The Manifesto states that:
  • Human races exist;
  • There are minor races and major races;
  • The concept of race is purely biologic;
  • The current population of Italy is mostly arian and its civilization is arian;
  • The mixture of races within the italian peninsula in the last 1000 years is a legend;
  • There is a pure Italian race;
  • It’s time that Italians declare themselves as racists;
  • It’s necessary to make a distinction between Mediterraneans of Europe (Western) on the one side, and Eastern as well as Africans on the other side.
  • Jewish people don’t belong to the Italian race;
  • Purely European physical and psychological characters of Italians cannot be altered in any way.
In the following months, Jewish people started being slowly excluded from public places and institutions. Marriages between Jewish and non-Jewish were prohibited and the possession of goods on behalf of Jewish citizens was also impeded. A census of all Italian Jewish was completed and special measures were enacted for Jewish foreigners living in the Italian peninsula. One of the first institutions to be ruled was the school: indeed, Jewish people were progressively excluded from teaching and studying in Italian public schools and universities.
In September 1938, the government was forced to deal with the starting academic year in relation to racial dispositions. Even though the official RDL (Regio Decreto Legge or Royal Decree-Law) n.1728 on the “Measures for the defense of the Italian race” was only issued on November 17th, 1938, schools and universities needed to be reformed beforehand in order to already apply the ideas mentioned in Manifesto earlier in July 1938. The first RDL in this sense was issued on September 5th, 1938 as n.1390: “Measures for the defense of the race in fascist schools”. With this Decree, Jewish people were excluded as teachers and university assistants in public and para-public schools: this procedure was effectively enacted on October 16th, 1938. This especially impacted Italian universities, since 7% of the teachers were Jewish (in comparison with the 1% Jewish people in the whole Italian population). Also, Jewish people were denied the possibility to apply to public schools or universities, and only people already enrolled in previous years were able to complete their course of studies. However, this possibility was not always easily granted: indeed, in the case of Ibolya/Violetta Barnai, the University of Bologna initially denied her application as a student for the academic year 1938-39.
On November 15th, 1938, another RDL was issued: all Jewish students were prohibited to attend Italian universities, with the exception of the ones that were already subscribed during the academic year 1937-38 and had taken all their exams on time. At the time, the definition of “Jewish” was still blurry: in Italy, one was considered to be Jewish if at least one of their parents was. It was not necessary to be actively practicing the religion: in fact, the sole fact of belonging to Jewish descent was considered enough to be excluded from institutions and public life in general. In this sense, the condition of Italian Jewish and foreign Jewish was equal: their nationality did not matter because their “race” was considered as their most important trait. Already on August 9th, 1938, universities in Italy were asked to compile a census of their staff and on the documents it was specified whether one member belonged to the jewish race. This aspect also regards the documents of the students whose folders were analyzed in this project: indeed, their “Israelitic descent” or “Jewish race” is specified in most of their personal files.

Content of the project
We have faithfully transcribed all the documents diplomatically for the five students in our focus group, containing ninety-five pages of scanned documents collected by the Archivio storico dell’Università di Bologna. These documents witnessed the pursuit of education and the youthful years of the early twenties of our students, who traveled a long way from their hometown to Bologna, after tedious bureaucratic processes and trials and errors in that turbulent era. It is a pity that two pages of these precious documents have not been transcribed and translated successfully due to our lack of knowledge of the Romanian language: however, we may try to complete this missing part in the future. All the documents have been uploaded to our website in high-resolution picture files, along with the diplomatic transcription and translation presented as plain text. We have horizontally designed the layout, placing the original document, the transcription, and the translation side by side so that our users could read them while finding connections and making comparisons among them.
