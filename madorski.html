BIOGRAPHY

Anna Madorski was born on May 31st, 1913 in Harbin (China). Her parents were Schender Madorski, a bourgeois who originated from Rogachev (Belarus), and Ghitel. Both of them were Russian Jewish immigrants to Harbin, a city located in northeast China where many Russian Jewish families settled after 1898, when Russia was granted the concession to build the Chinese Eastern Railway under the Russo-Manchurian treaty.
Read more
Anna had spent her childhood and teenage years in Harbin. In 1929, at the age of 16, she graduated from the First Russian Commercial College in Harbin with full marks in all subjects. In October of 1933, Anna started her studies in Chemistry under the Faculty of Physical, Mathematical, and Natural Sciences of the University of Bologna. Four years later, she passed her final exam in Chemistry and graduated with the final mark corresponding to One Hundred and Eight out of One Hundred and Ten (108/110). Later in November of 1937, she chose to continue her studies in Pharmacy, based on exams and courses she finished for her degree in Chemistry, it is possible to shorten her study plan and get the Doctor's degree in Pharmacy in only one year.

No more information about Anna is traceable after she got her second degree from the University of Bologna on January 2nd of 1939. Some related information could be found about her cousin Marco Madorski, who was also a Russian Jewish student from Harbin and had majored in Medicines and Surgery at the same university from 1932 to November of 1938, during which he and Anna lived together on Via Rialto in Bologna. After graduation, Marco went back to Shanghai, China instead of Japanese-occupied Harbin with his Italian-Jewish fiancee Ivonne Cividali. During the Japanese occupation period, the living condition of Jewish people in Harbin was worsening, and the treatment was even more oppressive in World War II, when the Japanese  allied with Nazi Germany. Many Jews left Manchuria before the outbreak of World War II.

It is unknown if Anna had gone back to China with her cousin in 1939. Furthermore, among the tombstones list of Harbin Jewish Cemetery, neither Anna nor her parents’ name could be found, which makes the inference reasonable that they might have moved away from Harbin at some time.
